=== GMAT EXAM OVERVIEW ===
Full form: Graduate Management Admission Test
Conducting body: GMAC (Graduate Management Admission Council)
Current version: GMAT Focus Edition (launched November 2023)
Purpose: Admission to MBA/Masters programs worldwide
Frequency: Can be taken anytime (online or test center)
Total attempts: 5 per rolling 12 months, max 8 lifetime
Score validity: 5 years
Eligibility: No specific degree requirement; typically taken by graduates
Test centers: Available in 100+ countries, 600+ test centers
Also available: Online proctored test from home
Score range: 205-805 (GMAT Focus Edition)
Accepted by: 2,400+ business schools worldwide

=== GMAT FOCUS EDITION EXAM PATTERN ===
Total Sections: 3 (45 minutes each)
Total Duration: 2 hours 15 minutes + breaks

Section 1 - Quantitative Reasoning:
- 21 questions, 45 minutes
- Problem Solving only (no Data Sufficiency — moved to DI section)
- Topics: Arithmetic, Algebra, Word Problems, Geometry, Number Properties
- Difficulty: Adaptive — questions get harder/easier based on performance

Section 2 - Verbal Reasoning:
- 23 questions, 45 minutes
- Critical Reasoning (CR) and Reading Comprehension (RC) only
- NO Sentence Correction in GMAT Focus Edition
- CR: Strengthen, Weaken, Assumption, Inference, Evaluate, Boldface
- RC: Main idea, Detail, Inference, Tone, Structure questions

Section 3 - Data Insights:
- 20 questions, 45 minutes
- Data Sufficiency (DS): moved here from Quant section
- Multi-Source Reasoning, Table Analysis, Graphics Interpretation
- Two-Part Analysis
- Mix of quantitative and verbal data analysis

Scoring:
- Each section scored 60-90 (in 1-point increments)
- Total composite score: 205-805
- Section order: Student's choice (choose at start of exam)
- No penalty for wrong answers (no negative marking)
- Computer Adaptive: difficulty adjusts per question

=== GMAT FOCUS VS OLD GMAT ===
Changes in Focus Edition:
- Removed AWA (Analytical Writing Assessment) — no essay
- Removed Sentence Correction from Verbal
- Data Sufficiency moved to Data Insights section
- Shorter exam: 2h15m vs 3h7m
- Score range: 205-805 vs 200-800
- Can review and change up to 3 answers per section
- Bookmark questions to revisit
- Choose section order before starting

=== GMAT SYLLABUS DETAILS ===
Quantitative Reasoning:
- Arithmetic: Number properties, fractions, decimals, percentages, ratios
- Algebra: Linear equations, quadratic equations, inequalities, functions
- Word Problems: Rate/work, mixtures, interest, profit/loss, age problems
- Geometry: Lines, angles, triangles, circles, quadrilaterals, coordinate geometry
- Statistics: Mean, median, mode, standard deviation, range
- Counting and Probability: Permutations, combinations, basic probability
- No calculus, no trigonometry, no advanced mathematics

Verbal Reasoning:
Critical Reasoning:
- Argument structure: Premise, Conclusion, Assumption
- Strengthen the argument
- Weaken the argument
- Find the assumption
- Draw an inference
- Evaluate the argument
- Boldface questions (identify role of statements)
- Paradox/Explain questions

Reading Comprehension:
- Passages: Business, social science, biological science, physical science
- Question types: Main idea, Supporting idea, Inference, Application, Tone
- Passage length: Short (200-250 words) and Long (300-350 words)

Data Insights:
- Data Sufficiency: Given a question and two statements, determine if sufficient
- Multi-Source Reasoning: Analyze data from multiple tabs/sources
- Table Analysis: Sort and analyze data tables
- Graphics Interpretation: Read graphs, charts, and fill in statements
- Two-Part Analysis: Two-component answer in a table format

=== COMMON GMAT QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS ===
Q: What is a good GMAT score?
A: 700+ is competitive for top global MBA programs. Average GMAT at top schools: HBS 730, Wharton 733, Stanford 738, INSEAD 710, ISB 710. However, GMAT is one part of a holistic application. A 680 with exceptional work experience and essays can beat a 760 with a weak profile.

Q: GMAT vs GRE — which should I take?
A: GMAT is preferred by top B-Schools and signals commitment to MBA. GRE is accepted by most B-Schools now but some admissions committees still prefer GMAT. If you are 100% focused on MBA, take GMAT. If you want flexibility to apply to other graduate programs too, GRE might be better.

Q: How long to prepare for GMAT?
A: Most candidates need 3-4 months of dedicated preparation (2-3 hours daily). With strong quant background, 2 months may suffice. Working professionals typically take 4-6 months. Take a diagnostic first — your baseline score determines how much time you need.

Q: Is GMAT Focus Edition easier than old GMAT?
A: Not necessarily easier, but different. Removing Sentence Correction helps some. Moving Data Sufficiency to DI changes strategy. The shorter duration (2h15m vs 3h7m) means less fatigue. The ability to review/change answers is new and powerful. Overall, similar difficulty but better testing experience.

Q: Can I prepare for GMAT without coaching?
A: Yes, many 700+ scorers are self-prepared. Use Official Guide (OG), GMAT Club forums, and official practice tests. However, coaching provides strategy frameworks, structured preparation, and accountability. At minimum, take official mock tests from mba.com.

Q: What is Data Sufficiency and how to approach it?
A: DS gives you a question and two statements. You must determine if the statements (individually or together) provide sufficient information to answer — you do NOT need to solve completely. Key approach: Use the AD/BCE method. If Statement 1 alone is sufficient, answer is A or D. If not, check Statement 2 alone.

Q: How many times can I take the GMAT?
A: Up to 5 times in a rolling 12-month period, maximum 8 lifetime attempts. There must be a 16-day gap between attempts. Schools see all scores unless you cancel. Most improvement happens between 1st and 2nd attempts.

Q: What is the GMAT scoring algorithm?
A: GMAT Focus is computer-adaptive at the section level. Your initial questions establish a difficulty baseline. Correct answers lead to harder questions (higher potential score). The final score considers difficulty level and accuracy. You can bookmark and review up to 3 changes per section.

Q: When should I apply after getting my GMAT score?
A: Most MBA programs have Round 1 (Sep-Oct), Round 2 (Jan), Round 3 (Mar-Apr). Round 1 and 2 are best for scholarships. Take GMAT at least 2-3 months before application deadlines to have time for essays and recommendations. GMAT scores are valid for 5 years.
